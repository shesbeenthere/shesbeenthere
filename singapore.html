Singapore

Top 10 Things to Do and See

1. Marina Bay Sands

This 57-story world-renowned hotel has amazing views of both the city and the bay. Marina Bay Sands is comprised of three separate towers connected by a 57th-floor roof terrace, complete with a full-service restaurant and bar, and a world famous infinity pool overlooking the city. This hotel tops the charts on the list of places to stay in Singapore and is sure to leave unforgettable memories.

 

2. Sentosa Island

Sentosa Island is a small island connected to Singapore. It is Asia’s leading leisure destination and has endless activities for family fun. From zip-lining to indoor skydiving, and nightclubs to beach bars, Sentosa Island has everything you’ve ever dreamed of doing and more.

 

 

3. Gardens by the Bay

Gardens by the Bay is a magnificent garden comprised of 12 different attractions. Among its attractions is the famous Super Tree Grove. Reaching heights of 165 feet, the artificial trees are designed to help create a more sustainable community. These solar-powered “super trees” provide shade to the garden’s visitors during the day and a magnificent light show at night. Some of the garden’s other attractions include a skyway, conservatory and sun pavilion.

 

4. Singapore Botanic Gardens

Developed by Stamford Raffles himself, Singapore’s Botanic Gardens contain more than 150 years of history and are one among many UNESCO World Heritage Sites around the world. The Botanic Gardens contain 183 acres of beautiful scenery, plants and flowers, and includes one of the region’s first rubber tree orchards.

 

5. Singapore Flyer

The Singapore flyer is the largest observation wheel in Asia and is considered one of Asia’s biggest tourist attractions. The wheel stands at 541 feet or 42 stories high and provides an amazing view of downtown Singapore. While on board, guests can enjoy a famous Singapore Sling or elegant meal from the Sky View Kitchen.

 

 

6. MacRitchie Nature Trail

MacRitchie Park is the perfect place for those nature enthusiasts who love to explore nature and venture outdoors. The 30-acre park comes complete with a beautiful waterfront for evening walks or morning strolls. Canoes and kayaks are also available for rent. Along with the lake, the park is home to thousands of monkeys and even includes a treetop walk across the forest’s luscious canopy.

 

7. Raffles Hotel

At 128 years old, Raffles Hotel is one of the most beautiful architectural sites that Singapore has to offer. The name Raffles carries with it the idea of luxury and glamor. In modern times, Raffles Hotel is known for its stunning construction and is home to the original Singapore Sling.

 

 

8. Singapore Zoo's Night Safari

Singapore’s Night Safari is the first nocturnal wildlife park in the world. It opened in 1994 and welcomes over 1 million visitors per year. This award-winning park takes you on a journey — via tram rides — to observe over 2,500 animals in their natural nighttime habitat.

 

 

9. East Coast Park

Totaling nearly 10 miles long, Singapore’s East Coast Park is the perfect place to enjoy the outdoors. From rollerblading and cycling to oceanfront restaurants, East Coast Park has everything you need to enjoy the outdoors of beautiful Singapore. It’s the picture-perfect setting to get a taste of traditional Singaporean food and local life. 

 

10. Chinatown

Chinatown is the place to go if you want to immerse yourself in traditional Chinese cultural. Comprised of 5 districts, Chinatown has everything: traditional cafes and shops, mosques and temples, hotels and hawkers and even a cluster of historical associations. 

 

 

History

Early Singapore: A Brief History

Much of Singapore’s history has been lost with time. Little to no information about this island is known before the late 13th century. Some of the earliest recorded settlements date back to 1298 AD in the city known as Temasek or “Sea Town.” 

Legend has it that the Prince of Indonesia was on a hunting excursion when he caught site of an unfamiliar animal.  Taking this as a sign of good luck, he went back to the place where he spotted this animal and founded a city. He named this city Singapura which translates into “The Lion City.”

In the years following, Singapura was ruled by five ancient kings who transformed the city into a meeting point of sea roots and a flourishing trading post for surrounding countries.
In the late 18th century, the British began to take control of this region. They saw a perfect opportunity to establish their own trading post, as well as protect their merchant fleet from any threats by neighboring countries. A new empire was born and the country began to prosper.

As the early 19th century began, Britain offered a series of proposals that granted Singapore greater measure of self-government. On August 9th, 1965, Singapore became a sovereign, democratic and independent nation.

Modern Singapore: Independence

In 1953, Britain began to nudge Singapore in the direction of self-government and independence. Two short years later in 1955,  the first election was held and a new government was formed. The countries newest challenge was learning how to be sovereign. Under the leadership of Lee Kuan Yew and the PAP (People’s Action Party), the nation began to flourish. Trade between Japan and the United States increased and Singapore became a major shipping port.

In 1967, Singapore joined ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations) to help continue to promote regional stability, economic development, and cultural exchange.


By the early 1970’s Singapore had become a world leader in shipping, air transport and oil refining.
On August 9th, 1965, Singapore separated itself from Malaysia and started on the road towards independence.
Present day Singapore continues to flourish both economically and culturally. They are among the most successful countries in Southeast Asia and continue to lead in the transportation and distribution of freight and cargo around the world. 

Food

Traditional Cuisine:
Singaporean cuisine contains influence from many Southeast Asian countries. Heavily influenced by China and Indonesia, Singaporean food also contains Indian, Western, Sri Lankan, Thai and Middle Eastern influences. Most restaurants around Singapore are quite pricey compared to the United States and the countries surrounding Singapore. Because of this, many locals and tourists are drawn to the unique Singaporean hawker markets that are scattered around the city.


What is a hawker?
Hawker centers are giant open-air food courts with dozens of stalls selling fresh, local cuisine. Each stall offers its own specialty dish. Many hawker markets are outdoors and contain a variety of dishes from various cultures.


Local Food:
There are many local dishes that are native to Singapore. Like most Asian Countries, meats and fish served with rice are often common meals consumed in Singapore. Chicken rice, offered at most hawker centers, is made up of sticky white rice, poached chicken and a traditional sauce — usually comprised of garlic, ginger and pandan leaves.

Chili crab and black pepper crab are two other famous Singaporean dishes. Long Beach Restaurant in Singapore’s East Coast Park, is famous for its fresh Chili crab and tasty black pepper crab

Transportation


MRT (Mass Rapid Transit)
Singapore’s MRT is similar to Western light rails and trains. The MRT is almost entirely underground. It spans nearly 106 miles and contains 102 stations. Frequent commuters can purchase prepaid cards that can be scanned at all access gates. Single trip tickets can also be purchased at any of the ticketing stations located at each stop.


 

To learn more or calculate MRT fares visit: https://www.mytransport.sg/content/mytransport/home/commuting/trainmap.html


SBS (Singapore Bus Service)
Along with the MRT, Singapore has excellent public transportation via buses. The bus system has approximately 4,200 buses in operation and transports nearly 3.8 million riders each day. Singapore’s buses consist of single deck and double deck vehicles that make traveling around the city easy and affordable. Just as the MRT offers prepaid cards, so does the SBS. Travelers can also pay per trip via coins.

 

To learn more about bus routes and services visit: https://www.sbstransit.com.sg


Taxis
Another popular form of transportation in Singapore is a taxi cab. Taxis may be flagged down on many streets or cued up in busy places such as shopping centers. Compared to most cities in developed countries, taxi cab fares in Singapore are relatively cheap and affordable. Taxi fares are metered and it is common courtesy to tip the drivers after each ride.

 


To learn more about taxi fares and booking instructions visit: http://www.taxisingapore.com/taxi-fare/

 

Currency

History:
1939: Singapore began using the Malaysian dollar.

1945: Singapore currency changed to the Straits Dollar — the currency used by most British colonies.

1967: The very first Singaporean dollars —the “orchids”— were minted.

1976: The “orchids” were withdrawn from circulation and replaced with the “bird”.

1984: The ship series was introduced, along with a 2 dollar note.  

1999: The portrait currency —which is still in use today— is introduced.

 

The current currency contains portraits of important figures in Singapore history.

The portrait currency, which many have said looks and feels similar to play money, comes in 2, 5, 10, 50, 100, 1,000 and 10,000 dollar notes.


The front of each note features the Singapore Arms, a watermark of the portrait, the Singapore Lion symbol, the word Singapore in the four different languages, and the signature and seal of the Chairman, BCCS or Chairman, MAS. The back of each note features a theme. The themes include Education, Garden City, Sports, Arts, Youth, Government and Economics.
